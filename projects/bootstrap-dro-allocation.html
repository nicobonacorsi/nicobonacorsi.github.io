Abstract

This study aggregates the Kenneth French 100 size–book-to-market portfolios into a continuous 25-portfolio panel using lagged capitalization weights. At each monthly rebalance, circular-block bootstrap resampling estimates dispersion in the sample eigenvector directions. Directional upper quantiles generate a positive-semidefinite covariance matrix for an exact capped long-only quadratic program. Hyperparameters are selected on 2000–2012 and evaluated over 2013–2026.

The resulting portfolio records 16.45% annualized volatility, compared with 20.15% for equal weight and 16.46% for validation-selected Ledoit–Wolf GMV. The relative volatility reduction versus equal weight is 18.39%, with a paired block-bootstrap 95% interval of 14.95–22.58%. Performance is essentially tied with Ledoit–Wolf, while the bootstrap estimator provides an explicit directional decomposition of covariance uncertainty.

Research design

The two studies in the full report follow the same quantitative architecture: estimate uncertainty, preserve temporal causality, and embed the estimate directly in the decision rule. In the allocation model, bootstrap dispersion modifies the covariance matrix before optimization. The empirical design combines fixed validation and evaluation periods, explicit transaction-cost accounting, dependence-aware inference, benchmark portfolios, and machine-checkable temporal audits.

Data and accounting conventions

The allocation study uses the Kenneth French daily 100 size–book-to-market portfolio archive. Raw snapshots and SHA-256 hashes are included in the accompanying repository. Portfolio returns include explicit turnover costs.

Construction of a continuous characteristic panel

The source archive contains 100 value-weighted portfolios on a \(10\times10\) size–book-to-market grid. Extreme cells may be empty for an entire portfolio-formation year and are represented by missing-value codes. Dropping every day on which any cell is empty would introduce artificial gaps. Instead, adjacent \(2\times2\) cells are aggregated into 25 portfolios.

Let \(R_{i,t}\) denote the return of cell \(i\), \(N_{i,t}\) its firm count, and \(S_{i,t}\) its reported average firm size. For an aggregated group \(g\), the implemented return is

\[ R_{g,t} = \frac{ \sum_{i\in g:\,R_{i,t}\ \mathrm{observed}} M_{i,t-1}R_{i,t} }{ \sum_{i\in g:\,R_{i,t}\ \mathrm{observed}} M_{i,t-1} }, \qquad M_{i,t-1}=N_{i,t-1}S_{i,t-1}. \tag{1} \]

Empty cells receive zero weight. The capitalization measure is lagged, so same-day returns do not affect aggregation weights. The result is a complete 25-column panel from July 1963 through May 2026.

Directional bootstrap ambiguity set

At a rebalance date \(t\), let \(X_t\in\mathbb R^{L\times p}\) be the trailing return matrix and write the sample covariance as

\[ S_t = U_t\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_{1,t},\ldots,\lambda_{p,t})U_t^\top. \tag{2} \]

Circular moving-block resamples preserve short-range temporal dependence. For each bootstrap draw \(b\), the resampled data are projected onto the fixed sample eigenvectors, producing directional variances \(\lambda^{*(b)}_{j,t}\). For quantile level \(q\),

\[ \bar\lambda_{j,t}(q) = \max\left\{ \lambda_{j,t}, Q_q\!\left( \lambda^{*(1)}_{j,t},\ldots,\lambda^{*(B)}_{j,t} \right) \right\}, \tag{3} \] \[ \bar\Sigma_t(q) = U_t\operatorname{diag} \left( \bar\lambda_{1,t},\ldots,\bar\lambda_{p,t} \right)U_t^\top. \tag{4} \]

This matrix is the worst element, for every feasible long-only portfolio, in the rectangular set

\[ \mathcal U_t(q) = \left\{ U_t\operatorname{diag}(d)U_t^\top: \lambda_{j,t}\le d_j\le\bar\lambda_{j,t}(q) \right\}. \tag{5} \]

The set is positive semidefinite by construction and inflates estimated variance in directions supported by bootstrap dispersion. The specification isolates eigenvalue uncertainty in the sample eigenspace, producing a directional and economically interpretable risk adjustment.

Optimization and trading rule

Let \(w_t^-\) be the pre-trade portfolio obtained by drifting the previous target weights through realized returns. The monthly target solves

\[ \min_{w\in\mathbb R^p} \quad w^\top \frac{\widehat\Sigma_t}{\operatorname{tr}(\widehat\Sigma_t)/p} w + \gamma\lVert w-w_t^-\rVert_2^2, \tag{6} \] \[ \mathbf 1^\top w=1, \qquad 0\le w_i\le c. \tag{7} \]

The trace normalization fixes the scale of the displacement penalty across covariance estimates. A specialized active-set solver is used; the supplied test suite compares its objective value with a generic SLSQP solution to tolerance \(10^{-9}\).

The validation grid is \(L\in\{252,504\}\), \(q\in\{0.75,0.90,0.95\}\), \(c\in\{0.10,0.20\}\), and \(\gamma\in\{0,0.5,2\}\). The unique objective is minimum net annualized volatility over 2000–2012. The selected configuration is

\[ L=252,\qquad q=0.75,\qquad c=0.20,\qquad \gamma=0, \]

with 21-day blocks and 128 bootstrap replications at each rebalance. Since validation selects \(\gamma=0\), turnover is controlled through monthly rebalancing, weight caps, and net-of-cost model selection rather than an explicit displacement penalty.

At each rebalance, the strategy pays

\[ C_t = 10^{-3} \sum_{i=1}^p \left| w^{\mathrm{target}}_{i,t}-w^-_{i,t} \right|, \tag{8} \]

which is 10 basis points per dollar traded under the full \(L^1\) weight-change convention.

Sensitivity and risk calibration

Changing the estimator block length from 5 to 63 days and the number of bootstrap replications from 64 to 256 leaves evaluation volatility between 16.44% and 16.45%. The mean \(L^1\) distance from the primary target weights remains below 0.013. At 50 basis points per dollar traded, CAGR falls from 12.90% to 12.33%, while volatility is essentially unchanged.

The mean next-month realized-to-predicted volatility ratio is 0.941 for the bootstrap covariance and 0.998 for Ledoit–Wolf. The directional upper covariance is conservative on average. Forecast dispersion remains substantial during abrupt volatility transitions, highlighting the value of dynamic regime information beyond a static monthly covariance estimate.

Reproducibility and audit controls

The repository regenerates every reported table and figure from immutable public-data snapshots in a Python 3.12 environment through a single execution script. The audit suite verifies:

  • SHA-256 verification of all five raw data snapshots;
  • feasibility tests for capped-simplex projections and selected portfolio weights;
  • an active-set QP objective comparison against SLSQP at \(10^{-9}\) tolerance;
  • positive-semidefinite and trace-inflation checks for bootstrap covariance matrices;
  • continuity and completeness checks for the 25-portfolio panel;
  • strict future-target and label-maturity checks;
  • overlap checks requiring calibration observations to come from the same interlaced stream with disjoint target windows;
  • accounting identities for all-cash and all-SPY strategies;
  • non-empty artifact checks for every generated output.

All ten integrity tests pass, and the raw-data manifest verifies every snapshot without a hash mismatch. The tests cover data continuity, optimization feasibility, temporal causality, calibration overlap, accounting identities, and artifact generation.

Scope and research extensions

The current allocation experiment uses academic characteristic portfolios, which provide a long and internally consistent panel for comparing covariance estimators. A constituent-level implementation would add security-specific spreads, impact, capacity, taxes, and point-in-time membership.

The present ambiguity set perturbs eigenvalues in a fixed sample eigenspace. Joint eigenvalue–eigenvector uncertainty and structural-break adaptation define the next extension. A natural refinement is a simultaneous confidence region for portfolio variance over the capped simplex, replacing coordinatewise directional quantiles with a set calibrated directly to uniform risk underestimation. Constituent-level point-in-time data would then allow the displacement penalty and rebalance frequency to be selected under nonlinear execution costs.

Conclusion

The allocation study integrates bootstrap-estimated directional uncertainty into an exact convex portfolio problem. The resulting portfolio reduces annualized volatility by 18.39% relative to equal weight and matches the risk performance of Ledoit–Wolf GMV while preserving an interpretable decomposition of covariance uncertainty.

The contribution is not a claim of economically large dominance over Ledoit–Wolf. It is a reproducible workflow in which covariance uncertainty is estimated explicitly, audited for temporal validity, and carried through to a portfolio decision with transaction-cost accounting and dependence-aware inference.
